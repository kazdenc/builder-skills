# Strategy Plane

The foundation. Everything above this plane depends on getting strategy right. Strategy answers two questions: **What do we want to get out of this product?** and **What do our users want to get out of it?**

## Product Objectives

Define what the product should accomplish for the organization. Not features — outcomes.

### Getting Objectives Right

- **Too vague**: "Make money" or "Be useful" — doesn't guide decisions
- **Too specific**: "Provide real-time text communications" — that's a solution, not an objective
- **Right level**: "Reduce customer support calls by 25%" — clear outcome, doesn't prescribe how

Objectives should define conditions for success without defining the path to get there.

### Brand Identity

Brand isn't just logos and colors — it's the set of conceptual associations and emotional reactions users have about your organization. Every interaction with your product shapes brand perception, whether you design for it or not.

Ask: What impression should this product create? Trustworthy? Innovative? Approachable? Premium? This becomes an explicit objective that guides decisions across all planes.

### Success Metrics

Define how you'll know the strategy is working. Metrics must be:

- **Tied to user behavior** that design can influence
- **Measurable** after launch
- **Specific** enough to be actionable

Examples:
- Conversion rate (not just sales — isolates UX from marketing)
- Return visits (are users getting value?)
- Task completion time (is it getting easier?)
- Support ticket volume (are problems being solved?)

**Watch out**: Metrics in isolation mislead. A spike in time-on-site could mean engagement or confusion. Always consider context.

## User Needs

You are not your user. Research actual needs rather than projecting your own.

### User Segmentation

Break the audience into groups with shared needs:

- **Demographic**: Age, income, education, role
- **Psychographic**: Attitudes, values, comfort with technology
- **Behavioral**: How they use the product, frequency, expertise level

You only need as many segments as you have meaningfully different sets of needs. If two groups have the same needs, combine them.

**Critical**: Sometimes segments have conflicting needs. A novice wants step-by-step guidance; an expert wants rapid access to all functions. You must choose: serve one segment, or provide separate paths. This decision cascades through every plane above.

### User Research Methods

| Method | Best For | Depth | Scale |
|--------|----------|-------|-------|
| Surveys | General attitudes, preferences | Low | High |
| Interviews | Motivations, mental models | High | Low |
| Contextual inquiry | Real-world behavior | Very high | Very low |
| Task analysis | Understanding workflows | High | Low |
| User testing | Evaluating designs | Medium | Medium |
| Card sorting | Information organization | Medium | Medium |
| Analytics | Behavioral patterns | Medium | Very high |

### Personas

Turn research data into fictional characters that represent real user segments:

- Give them a name, photo, background
- Include their goals, frustrations, context of use
- Reference them during design decisions: "Would this work for [persona]?"

Personas keep the team focused on real people rather than abstract data points.

## Strategy Document

Capture strategy in a living document:

- Product objectives with rationale
- User needs with supporting research
- Personas
- Success metrics
- Priorities and trade-offs

**Keep it concise.** A 200-page strategy document won't be read. A focused 5-10 page document that the whole team references actively is far more valuable.

**Don't lock it away.** Every team member — designer, developer, PM — needs access to make informed decisions.

## Common Strategy Failures

- **No explicit strategy** — Everyone has a different idea of what they're building
- **Strategy set in stone** — Refusing to adapt as you learn more about users
- **Metrics without context** — Optimizing a number without understanding what drives it
- **Ignoring conflicting needs** — Trying to serve everyone equally, serving no one well
- **Jumping to solutions** — Defining features before understanding the problem
