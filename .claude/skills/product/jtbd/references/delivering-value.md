# Delivering Value

Getting the solution right is half the problem. How you put it in people's hands — onboarding, support, retention — determines whether the job actually gets done.

## The Consumption Journey

Different from the job map. A job map shows what performers are trying to accomplish independent of any solution. The consumption journey shows how they find, choose, adopt, and stick with yours.

### Stages to Map

| Stage | What they're doing | What to look at |
|-------|-------------------|-----------------|
| **Discover** | Learning solutions exist | How do they find out about you? What triggers the search? |
| **Evaluate** | Comparing options | What criteria? Who influences them? What do they try? |
| **Acquire** | Signing up or purchasing | What friction exists? How long does it take? What almost stops them? |
| **Start** | Getting to first value | How quickly can they do something real? What blocks them? |
| **Use** | Getting the core job done | Does it actually satisfy the main job needs? Where does it fall short? |
| **Maintain** | Keeping it working | How much ongoing effort? What breaks? |
| **Grow** | Expanding usage | How do their needs evolve? What new stages of the job do they want covered? |
| **Leave** | Stopping or switching | What triggers it? What would have kept them? |

For each stage: note the touchpoints, the pain, the emotional state, and where you're losing people.

## Onboarding

The gap between signing up and getting the job done is where most products lose people. Close it fast.

### The Goal

Not "teach the product." The goal is: **get them to the job outcome as quickly as possible.**

What's the smallest piece of the job they can complete in their first session? Start there.

### Patterns That Work

- **Smart defaults over configuration.** Don't make them choose 15 settings before they can start. Pick good defaults, let them adjust later.
- **Real content over empty states.** Pre-populate with their actual data, or at minimum, realistic examples. An empty screen with a "get started" button is a dead end.
- **Progress toward the job, not product setup.** Frame completion in terms of what they're accomplishing ("Your first report is ready") not what they've configured ("Setup 3 of 7 complete").
- **Learning in context, not upfront tours.** Nobody remembers a 10-step feature walkthrough. Show guidance at the moment they need it.

### Patterns That Fail

| Anti-Pattern | Why it fails |
|-------------|-------------|
| Feature tour on first login | They don't have context yet — nothing sticks |
| Configuration wizard | Delays the job. They came to do something, not to set something up |
| "Watch this 5-minute video" | Adds friction before any value. Most will skip or bounce |
| Empty dashboard | No signal about what to do. Feels broken |

### Measuring It

- **Time to first value** — Minutes/hours from signup to completing a meaningful step of their job
- **Activation rate** — Percentage who reach the moment where the product clicks
- **First-session return** — Do they come back? If not, the first experience didn't land
- **Setup abandonment** — Where in onboarding do people drop out?

## Churn

People leave when the forces pushing them away outweigh the forces keeping them. Diagnose which forces are at play before trying to fix it.

### Diagnosing Churn with the Four Forces

| Force | In retention terms | Ask |
|-------|-------------------|-----|
| **Push** (away from you) | Growing frustration with your product | What's getting worse? What used to work but doesn't? |
| **Pull** (toward something else) | Attraction to an alternative | What are they leaving for? What does it promise? |
| **Habit** (keeping them) | Familiarity, switching costs, data lock-in | What would they lose by leaving? How embedded are you? |
| **Anxiety** (about leaving) | Fear of change | What worries them about switching? Data loss? Learning curve? |

If you're relying on habit and anxiety to retain people, you're not getting the job done — you're just making it hard to leave. That's not the same thing.

### Common Reasons People Leave

| Cause | What happened | What to do |
|-------|--------------|-----------|
| **The job changed** | Their needs evolved, your product didn't keep up | Continuously research how the job is shifting |
| **Overserved** | You do more than they need, they want something simpler | Offer a lighter tier. Don't force complexity |
| **Underserved** | Critical needs are still unmet despite using you | Prioritize the specific needs driving churn, not the loudest feature requests |
| **Circumstances shifted** | Company grew, role changed, budget cut | Build flexibility into pricing and feature access |
| **Emotional failure** | The product makes them feel stupid, frustrated, or embarrassed | Redesign the experience around confidence and competence |

## Support as Signal

Support tickets are research data. Every ticket is someone telling you where your product fails to get the job done.

### Reframing Support

**Traditional**: "How do I use feature X?"
**Through the job lens**: "I'm trying to [job stage] and I'm stuck."

Train support to understand the performer's job, not just the product's interface. When an agent understands what the person is actually trying to accomplish, they solve the real problem instead of just answering the surface question.

### Mining Support for Insight

Track patterns across tickets:

- **Which job stages generate the most tickets?** That's where the product has gaps.
- **What workarounds are people describing?** They're bending the product to fit their job — build what they're improvising.
- **What circumstances come up that you didn't design for?** People use products in contexts you didn't anticipate. Those are either expansion opportunities or boundaries to set.
- **What emotional language shows up?** Frustrated, confused, embarrassed, anxious — these point to emotional jobs you're failing.

When the same pattern appears across many tickets, it's not a support problem. It's a product problem. Feed it back to the team as a need with a job stage attached.
