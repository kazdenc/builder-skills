# Defining Value

Once you've talked to people and mapped the job, turn raw insight into strategic decisions. Don't brainstorm what customers need — derive it from what you heard.

## Scoring Needs

Not all needs are equal. Some are critical and unmet. Some are table stakes. Some are overserved — you're spending effort on things people don't care about. Separate them.

### How to Score

For each need statement, ask performers two questions (1-10 scale):
- **How important is this to you?**
- **How satisfied are you with how you handle it today?**

Then calculate:

```
Opportunity = Importance + max(Importance - Satisfaction, 0)
```

This weights importance twice when satisfaction is low — the formula surfaces needs that matter a lot and aren't being met.

| Score | What it means |
|-------|--------------|
| **15+** | Major gap. People care deeply and nothing serves them well. Build here. |
| **12-14** | Strong opportunity. Worth prioritizing. |
| **10-12** | Moderate. Consider if it fits your strategy. |
| **Below 10** | Adequately served or not important enough to differentiate on. |

### The Landscape

Plot needs on two axes to see the full picture:

```
           Matters a lot
                │
    BUILD HERE  │  TABLE STAKES
    (high need, │  (high need,
     low sat)   │   already met)
   ─────────────┼─────────────
    IGNORE      │  OVERSERVED
    (low need,  │  (low need,
     low sat)   │   high sat — cut costs?)
                │
           Doesn't matter
     Not satisfied ←──→ Well satisfied
```

**Overserved needs are strategic too.** If competitors are pouring resources into needs that don't matter much, you can strip those out, offer a simpler/cheaper solution, and win the segment that doesn't want the bloat.

## Building Personas Around Jobs

Forget demographics. Cluster people by what they're trying to accomplish and which needs they prioritize.

### How to Build Them

1. Look at your interview data and need scores
2. Find groups who rank the same needs as most critical
3. Name each group by behavior, not identity — "The Optimizer", "The First-Timer", "The Delegator"
4. For each persona, capture:
   - The main job as they see it
   - Their top 5-7 needs (with importance scores)
   - Key circumstances that shape their job
   - What they're using today and what's not working
   - Emotional and social dimensions

### What to Leave Out

- Demographics (unless a demographic directly creates a circumstance — e.g., "new parent" changes the job of "get to work on time")
- Stock photos and fictional backstories
- Psychographic filler ("Sarah values authenticity and connection")

A 22-year-old and a 55-year-old can share a persona if they're trying to get the same job done with the same priorities. That's the point.

## Competitive Analysis Through the Job

Stop comparing feature lists. Compare how well each option satisfies the needs within the job.

### How to Do It

1. Take your top 15-20 needs from research
2. Rate each competitor on how well they satisfy each need (use your interviews, reviews, your own assessment)
3. Look for patterns

| Need | You | Competitor A | Competitor B | Nobody |
|------|-----|-------------|-------------|--------|
| Minimize time to [x] | 4 | 8 | 3 | |
| Reduce effort to [y] | 6 | 5 | 7 | |
| Maximize likelihood of [z] | 2 | 2 | 2 | ← opportunity |

**The column that matters most is "Nobody."** Needs where no competitor scores well are your opening.

### Types of Competition

- **Direct** — Same kind of product for the same job (Figma vs. Sketch)
- **Indirect** — Different approach to the same job (spreadsheet vs. project management tool)
- **Non-consumption** — People doing nothing, tolerating the pain, using duct-tape workarounds

Non-consumption is often the biggest market. When people just deal with it, they're waiting for someone to make the switch worth it.

## Value Propositions

Connect what you're building to the jobs and needs you've identified. A value proposition that doesn't map to underserved needs is noise.

### The Structure

```
For [performers]
who need to [main job]
we [what you do differently]
so they can [top underserved needs you address]
unlike [current alternatives]
which [where they fall short]
```

### Principles

- **Lead with the job, not the product.** People don't care what you built. They care about getting the job done.
- **Be specific about which needs you serve.** "Save time" isn't a value prop. "Cut document gathering from 3 hours to 20 minutes" is.
- **Don't claim needs that are already well-served.** If competitors already satisfy a need, leading with it doesn't differentiate you.
- **Check that you can deliver.** A value proposition you can't back up is a marketing lie. Validate with your scoring data.

### Testing It

Ask yourself:
- Does this target the highest-scoring unmet needs from our research?
- Would a performer recognize this as solving their problem — in their words, not ours?
- Is this meaningfully different from what's already available?
- Can we actually build this?
