# Discovering Value

How to find jobs through research. You won't discover jobs from analytics dashboards or brainstorming sessions. You find them by talking to the people doing the work.

## Running Jobs Interviews

The goal is to understand what someone is trying to accomplish — not what they think of your product or what features they want.

### Who to Talk To

Talk to performers — the people actually doing the job. Not managers who think they know. Not buyers who purchase on behalf of others. Not internal stakeholders who "represent the voice of the customer."

You don't even need your own customers. In fact, people unfamiliar with your product often give cleaner signal because they won't anchor to your solution.

- **Minimum**: 5-6 interviews to start seeing patterns
- **Better**: 10-12 for solid confidence
- **Thorough**: 20+ when the stakes are high

### Screening

Write a short screening script. Three parts:
1. **Intro** — What the study is about (the job domain, never your product)
2. **Qualifier questions** — Confirm they actually do the job. Build in disqualifiers.
3. **Scheduling** — Book 60-90 minute slots. On-site is best, remote works.

Never mention your product, brand, or competitors in the screener.

### Running the Conversation

This is not a survey. Don't read questions off a list. Steer a conversation through these themes:

**Open with context.** "Walk me through how you [main job]. Start from the beginning — what triggers it?"

**Map the process.** "What do you do first? Then what? How do you know when you're done?" Follow their sequence, not yours.

**Dig into needs.** "What matters most to you when you're doing this? What does 'good' look like? What does 'bad' look like?"

**Find the pain.** "What's the hardest part? Where does it break down? What takes longer than it should?"

**Spot workarounds.** "How do you deal with that today?" Every workaround is a signal of an unmet need.

**Get the emotional layer.** "How do you feel when this goes well? When it doesn't? Does anyone else care about how this turns out?"

**Understand circumstances.** "When does this typically happen? What makes it harder? Easier? Are there situations where the whole thing changes?"

### What to Listen For

- **When they describe a workaround** — that's an unmet need
- **When they say "I just..." and describe something painful** — they've normalized the pain
- **When they mention other people** — that's the social job or the audience
- **When they get animated or frustrated** — you're near something important
- **When they describe switching between tools** — the job crosses solution boundaries

Translate everything into job language afterward. Their words → your framework.

## Switch Interviews

When you want to understand what makes someone change solutions — what pushed them away from the old thing and pulled them toward the new one.

### Walk the Timeline Backward

Start from the moment they switched, then trace back:

1. **The switch** — What did you switch to? When?
2. **The decision** — What made you finally pull the trigger?
3. **Active search** — When did you start actively looking? What did you compare?
4. **Passive awareness** — When did you first notice alternatives? What caught your eye?
5. **First thought** — When did you first think "this isn't working"? What was happening?

Then follow forward:

6. **After the switch** — How's it been? What surprised you? What do you miss?

### Key Questions

- "What was going on in your life/work when you first considered a change?"
- "What were you using before? What worked? What didn't?"
- "What was the final straw?"
- "What almost stopped you from switching?"
- "Did anyone else influence the decision?"

## The Four Forces

Every switching decision — adopting something new, leaving something old, choosing to do nothing — is shaped by four forces pulling in two directions.

```
  PUSHING TOWARD CHANGE              HOLDING BACK
  ────────────────────              ────────────────
  Frustration with today            Anxiety about the new thing
  (what's broken, what's slow,      (will it work? will I lose data?
   what's costing too much)          will I look stupid?)

  Attraction of the alternative     Comfort of the familiar
  (the promise, the demo,           (I know how this works,
   the friend's recommendation)      my stuff is here, it's fine)
```

**When the left side wins, people switch. When the right side wins, they stay put — even if your thing is objectively better.**

Use this to diagnose:
- **No adoption?** Either the push isn't strong enough (they don't feel the pain) or anxiety is too high (they're scared to try)
- **Slow adoption?** Habit is winning. Make switching effortless — migration tools, familiar patterns, low commitment entry points
- **Fast churn?** The pull was strong (good marketing) but the push wasn't real (the pain wasn't acute enough to sustain change)

After interviews, map each participant's forces. When you see the same forces across multiple people, you've found a pattern worth building on.

## Job Mapping

Create a visual map of the job to organize everything you've learned and spot where the opportunities are.

### The Natural Shape of a Job

Most jobs move through a predictable arc:

| Stage | What's happening |
|-------|-----------------|
| **Define** | Figure out what needs to get done, set objectives |
| **Locate** | Gather what's needed — inputs, information, materials |
| **Prepare** | Set up, get ready, arrange the environment |
| **Confirm** | Verify everything's in place before starting |
| **Execute** | Do the core work |
| **Monitor** | Track how it's going, check quality |
| **Adjust** | Course-correct based on what's happening |
| **Finish** | Wrap up, deliver the output, close out |

Not every job has all eight stages. Some have more. Use this as a starting scaffold, then adjust to match what you heard in interviews.

### Building the Map

1. Put the main job at the top
2. List the major stages left-to-right (the little jobs)
3. Under each stage, note specific steps people described
4. Attach needs to each stage — where did people describe pain, workarounds, or strong preferences?
5. Mark which stages are well-served today vs. underserved

### Reading the Map

- **Clusters of unmet needs** → That's where to build
- **Stages nobody serves** → Potential for a whole new category
- **Stages that are overserved** → Opportunity to simplify and undercut on price
- **The stages at the edges** (define, finish) → Often neglected by competitors who focus on the core "execute" stage

The job map becomes the team's shared reference point. When someone proposes a feature, point to the map: "Which stage does this serve? Which needs does it address?"
