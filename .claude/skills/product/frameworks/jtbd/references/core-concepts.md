# Core Concepts

## The Five Elements

Pull these apart every time. When someone describes a product idea, a feature request, or a customer problem, break it into these five pieces before responding.

### Who — The Performer

The person doing the job. Not the person paying, not the person approving, not the person consuming the output. The one with their hands on it.

Map out who's involved when it's not obvious:

| Role | They... | Example |
|------|---------|---------|
| **Performer** | Does the job | Developer writing code |
| **Buyer** | Pays for the solution | Procurement manager |
| **Approver** | Signs off on the purchase | VP with budget authority |
| **Manager** | Oversees the performer | Team lead setting practices |
| **Audience** | Receives the output | Stakeholder reading the report |
| **Assistant** | Helps the performer | Teammate pair-programming |

Start with the performer's needs. Always. The buyer matters, the audience matters — but the performer comes first.

Name them after the job: "attend a conference" → conference attendee. "Prepare a meal" → meal preparer. Keep it simple.

### What — The Job

The goal, stated without any reference to how they'll accomplish it.

**The test**: Would this statement make sense 50 years ago? If it mentions an app, a database, a workflow tool — it's not a job, it's a solution.

**Types to separate:**
- **Main job** — The functional objective. This is the anchor. "Prepare a meal", "file taxes", "hire a contractor."
- **Related jobs** — Adjacent goals that compete for time and attention. They're not stages of the main job, they're different jobs that collide with it.
- **Emotional jobs** — How they want to feel. "Feel confident the house is secure." Always start with "feel."
- **Social jobs** — How they want to be perceived. "Be seen as someone who makes smart decisions."

**Sequence for making product decisions**: Get the functional job done first. Then layer emotional. Then social. Jumping to emotional without solving the functional job is how you get products that feel good but don't work.

### How — The Process

The job has stages. Think of each stage as a smaller job within the main job — not as a task or a click, but as a sub-objective.

When mapping a job's process, aim for 5-10 stages with a clear beginning and end. Each stage follows the same writing rules as a job statement.

A job map is not a journey map. Job maps show what people are trying to accomplish regardless of what product they use. Journey maps show how people interact with your specific product.

### Why — The Needs

How the performer judges whether the job is going well. These are the success criteria, not feature requests.

Write each need as: `direction + measure + object + clarifier`

| Direction | Measure | Object | Clarifier |
|-----------|---------|--------|-----------|
| Minimize | the time it takes | to select a conference | to attend |
| Maximize | the likelihood | of remembering key content | from sessions |
| Reduce | the effort required | to share takeaways | with the team |

**Rules:**
- One need per statement. If there's an "and" or "or", split it.
- Prefer "minimize" over "reduce" — zero is concrete, "maximized" is vague.
- A single main job will have 50-150 needs. That's normal.
- Needs are not feature requests. "I need a search bar" is a solution. "Minimize the time it takes to find relevant content" is a need.

### When/Where — Circumstances

The conditions that shape how the job plays out. Without context, a job is too abstract to act on.

"Get breakfast" is vague. "Get breakfast while commuting and running late" is specific enough to design for. The circumstances narrow the job to something actionable.

Look for factors around time, place, and constraints:
- When it's the first time doing this
- When budget is limited
- When working under a deadline
- When the stakes are high

## The Hierarchy

Jobs nest inside each other. Knowing which level you're operating at prevents two common mistakes: scoping too narrowly (building a parking app when the job is "get to a destination on time") or too broadly (trying to innovate on "live a fulfilling life").

| Level | What it is | Example |
|-------|-----------|---------|
| **Aspiration** | An ideal state — no clear "done" | Be a better parent |
| **Big job** | A broad functional objective | Visit family on holidays |
| **Little job** | A stage within the big job | Arrange transportation |
| **Micro-job** | A specific activity | Book a car rental |

**Navigate with two questions:**
- **"Why do they do this?"** → moves up. Why arrange transportation? To visit family.
- **"How do they do this?"** → moves down. How visit family? Arrange transportation, buy gifts, coordinate schedules.

**Where to aim**: One level broader than what you can currently deliver. That gives room to grow without being so abstract that you can't act.

If you catch yourself defining a job as "be a better professional" or "enjoy life more" — you've gone too high. Note the aspiration for positioning and marketing, then come back down to a functional job.

## Writing Jobs — Quick Checks

Run every job statement through these:

| Check | Pass? |
|-------|-------|
| Starts with a verb | |
| Reflects the performer's perspective, not the company's | |
| No technology, products, or methods mentioned | |
| No adjectives smuggling in needs (fast, easy, cheap) | |
| Has a clear end state — the job can be "done" | |
| Single concept — no "and" or "or" | |
| Would read the same 50 years ago | |

**Common rewrites:**

| Wrong | Problem | Rewrite |
|-------|---------|---------|
| Search for documents in the database | Method + technology | Retrieve content |
| Find the cheapest flights quickly | Needs baked in (cheap, quick) | Find flights |
| Help me plan a fun vacation | "Help me" prefix + need (fun) | Plan a vacation |
| Manage financial portfolio | No end state (managing is ongoing) | Grow financial portfolio |

## Scoping a Project

When starting any product thinking, work through these three steps with the team:

**1. Set the main job.** What is the customer trying to accomplish? Get the altitude right — broad enough to see opportunity, narrow enough to act on. Keep causal connection to your business (a drill bit company can't meaningfully innovate on "preserve family memories").

**2. Name the performer.** Who actually does this? Talk to a few of them informally before committing. If you can't find performers to talk to, your job definition might be off.

**3. Sketch the process and circumstances.** Hypothesize the stages. List the contextual factors you think matter. Hold these loosely — research will update them.
